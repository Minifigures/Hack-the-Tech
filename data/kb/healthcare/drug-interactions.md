# Drug Interactions

This module summarises clinically important drug interactions encountered in primary care.

## warfarin-sjw

St. John's wort (Hypericum perforatum) is a potent inducer of the cytochrome
P450 enzymes CYP3A4 and CYP2C9. Because warfarin is metabolised by these
enzymes, concurrent use lowers warfarin plasma concentrations, reduces the
International Normalised Ratio (INR), and increases the risk of thrombotic
events. The combination should be avoided. If unavoidable, clinicians must
increase INR monitoring and titrate the warfarin dose, and they must repeat
monitoring whenever the supplement is started, stopped, or changed in dose.

## warfarin-nsaid

Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) increase bleeding risk in
patients on warfarin through platelet inhibition and gastric mucosal injury,
even without changing the INR. Acetaminophen at doses below 2 g per day is
preferred for analgesia. Where an NSAID is required, the shortest duration and
the lowest effective dose should be used, with proton-pump inhibitor cover.

## ssri-tramadol

Concurrent use of SSRIs or SNRIs with tramadol increases serotonergic activity
and elevates the risk of serotonin syndrome. Document the indication, monitor
for autonomic instability and neuromuscular hyperactivity, and consider an
alternative analgesic where feasible.

## maoi-tyramine

Patients on irreversible MAO inhibitors must avoid tyramine-rich foods such as
aged cheeses, cured meats, and fermented soy products due to the risk of
hypertensive crisis.
